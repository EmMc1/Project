Why weather forecasts are important

The official name for the science of weather is Meteorology. It's the job of meteorologists to study weather patterns, and forecast (predict) weather and possible extreme weather.

Weather is all around us. It's the condition of the atmosphere. From the clouds, rain and snow, to wind, thunder and sunshine, weather affects all aspects of life, so understanding the science behind it is really important.
Weather also includes pressure in the air, temperature and humidity. Weather affects all parts of daily life. Therefore, it's important that we're able to track weather patterns and predict what weather we will experience and when. For example, poor weather conditions can lead to crop damage and flooding.

How weather data is collected

Meteorologists use different weather instruments to measure and record the weather. These instruments are usually located in weather stations placed around the country. Weather instruments are also located in the sea, on planes and within satellites.

    Here's a list of weather instruments:
  1. Thermometer
    A thermometer measures the temperature over a 24-hour period.
  2. Stevenson Screen
    These shelters contain the thermometers. This is because temperature needs to be measured in the shade and the thermometer is protected and raised off the ground.
  3. Rain Gauge
    his measures the amount of precipitation that has fallen in a 24-hour period.
  4. Anemometer
    An anemometer measures wind speed in miles or km per hour.Using these tools and a variety of methods, weather forecasters and meteorologists are able to predict future weather. This enables us to plan and prepare accordingly, so that we're not negatively impacted by weather conditions.

Climates around the world

Weather refers to the day to day conditions and changes in the atmosphere. Climate refers to the average weather conditions of a place over a long period of time.No two places on Earth have exactly the same climate. Nevertheless, many climates are similar to one another.


    Climates around the world
  1. Tropical
    Tropical climates are warm all year and have no winter. They lie near the Equator. Some tropical climates have a lot of rain while others are dry.
  2. Temperate
    Temperate climates are found mostly north of the Equator. In these climates, cold air from the north mixes with warm tropical air from the south. This mixing often causes rain and snow. Temperate climates usually have warmer summers and colder winters.
  3. Polar
    Polar climates are very cold. Snow and ice often cover the land. Some polar areas always have a layer of frost, called permafrost, below the soil.
  4. Highland
    Highland climates have a great range of temperature between day and night. They tend to be humid and cooler than the lower lands nearby.
